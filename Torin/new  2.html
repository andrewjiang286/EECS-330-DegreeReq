This is the first of a three-quarter sequence intended primarily for science and engineering majors and premedical students. PHYSICS 135-1 covers classical mechanics. Topics covered include: Newton's Laws of motion, projectiles, relative motion, circular motion and centripetal force, conservation of energy, momentum and angular momentum, center of mass, moment of inertia, and Newton's Law of gravity.

Prerequisites: Math 220-0, Math 224-0, Math 230-0 or equivalent

This is the second course in a three-quarter physics sequence intended for science and engineering majors and premedical students. Topics covered in 135-2 include electric and magnetic fields, electrical conduction, DC and AC circuits, capacitance, induction, and time-varying fields.

Prerequisites: Physics 135-1 or equivalent

Physics 135-3 is the third and final quarter of a calculus-based introductory physics course. Topics covered include mechanical waves, electromagnetic waves, geometric and wave optics, special relativity, and introductory concepts in quantum physics. It is intended for science majors, math majors, engineering majors, and premedical students.

Prerequisites: Physics 135-1,2 or equivalent

How do we engage the world politically? What does it mean to think politically? Is it even possible to be thoughtful in politics? This course explores the structures through which we approach these questions intellectually and in everyday life, to provide a series of lenses that can be used to view what is going on in the political world. As a way of introducing classical (and non-classical) texts as they relate to politics in everyday life, each week approaches a different kind of political problem, a problem of political action and reflection. Our focus will be on looking for resources in these texts for thinking differently about the political world that surrounds us, all the way down to the concept of politics itself and what counts as political and non-political.

Prerequisites: none

Games and gameplay occupy a special cultural space. Considered the province of children, many games have intricate patterns and subtleties that render their analysis both interesting and non-trivial. This course will be an introduction to the combinatorial game theory of Berlekamp, Conway, and Guy, which can be applied to perfect information, turn-based games like Domineering and Hackenbush as well as real life games like Nim, Konane, Hey! That's my Fish!, and (the endgame stages of) go/weiqi/baduk. We'll study comparability and addition of games. This course is intended for non-science students seeking a gentle introduction to the subject. While there will be technical details from time to time, this will not require the prerequisites of a more advanced course. Familiarity with high school mathematics is assumed.

Prerequisites: none

The integers: prime numbers and greatest common divisor, the fundamental theorem of arithmetic. Congruences: linear congruences, Chinese remainder theorem, applications. Special congruences: Wilson's theorem, Fermat's little theorem, pseudoprimes, Euler's theorem. Multiplicative functions: Euler phi function, perfect numbers, Mersenne primes. Primitive roots: order of an integer, existence of primitive roots; index arithmetic.

Prerequisites: MATH 230

Continuation of 336-1. Divisibility and primes, congruences, quadratic reciprocity, Diophantine problems. Additional topics in analytic and algebraic number theory.

Prerequisites: MATH 336-1
